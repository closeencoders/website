My AI is Bigger Than Yours

Posted Apr 18, 2026 | Tags: ai, rant, sarcasm

People are not afraid to share opinions on the internet, but they sure are afraid of falling behind other people who do the same thing.

Yet, it seems like anything of value, opinion or not, released into the wild, even with proper protection, licensing, and money to go to court if needed, is sucked up by a mass of web scrapers, bots, and patent trolls in pursuit of rent seeking or simply to not fall behind others doing the same.

I think some guy at a large company said it best, "Whoever is left standing with the biggest and baddest AI model will find the 'One Piece' and be king of the AI startups.”

Then they killed him on a big podium. I can't remember the details, please don't sue me.

Within my own echo chamber, I am not sure why anyone would want to train their AI using my code, or something similar, but I’m sure it has happened already. I envision a newbie coder prompting for the answer to a niche question for ChatGippity to respond with a solution similar to mine, with an O(n) that will continue looping until the eventual heat death of the universe.

Not because I want to sabotage anyone, but as I am learning, the first draft of my code is often “junk,” and I don’t go around claiming perfection or publishing it for any reason.

All jokes aside, I’m sure our bad practice projects would have been filtered out. Right?

Illusion of Creation

It's easier than ever to copy what prompts out of a LLM and spam it across the web. The output is obvious, giving a signature robot vibe from the “average” of every shared opinion, research paper, and project the model was trained on. It feels soulless because it lacks applied thinking and reflection from the creator, not because people used AI, but because they abused it.

Many professions now require you to compete with those who fully embrace AI at scale. Driving this, a subtle, but ever present gravitational pull toward the path of least effort for what is perceived to be the most output possible. With so much desire for a product that makes thinking “easy”, AI may continue to improve far into the future. The path of least resistance is not a suggestion, it's a law of nature and AI is not objectively bad, but the way in which it is used by some is not good to say the least.

Access to information and automation are the main pros of modern society, and you have already heard the obvious arguments against AI. The world has already proven that ethical, moral, and environmental concerns will not stop people from doing what they want, unfortunately. It’s the cliché irony in having advanced tools, but being too stupid to wield them safely, which seems to be a trademark of human nature. 

What I am worried about instead is the death of experts. Not the generic gatekeeping and annoying jerk behind a phoney degree or nepo-executive position “expert”. I’m talking about people who truly love their craft, hyper-fixate on learning, solve complex problems, and have the desire to pass this knowledge forward. The type of mentor I wish I had but never did.

Stuff that may never be found in an AI training dataset, or even be manually excluded, because it's a deep experience that matters only from the perspective of an original thinker who does not post on X, Reddit, and LinkedIn all day.

I am afraid that for a lot of people, the desire to “get good” at a craft, even just as a hobby, is going to die, because why bother if a robot or someone augmented with a robot can do it “good enough.”

Illusion of Ownership

The generic "Oh no! People will have no brain now" argument is a recurring theme. In the past, the same thing was said about search engines, and how Google is a monopoly on access to said information. The fear was the same, people will just "Google" the info they need and never “think” again.

In the case of search engines, however, you are forced to click on a link that takes you to a website where the alleged person who made the content (at least in theory) could get credit for what they did. Or if you have resources, go through some publishing process to help ensure your name is stamped on said thing or patented before you release it into the wild.

This feeble attempt at ownership is what drives much of (fortunately not all) the incentive to create and share value.

It's far from perfect, pirating and plagiarism dilute the idea of owning "digital media," but it works well enough that people still make software, write books, create content, and provide services online all the time.

Could AI simply output these sources for the given data after every prompt? This sounds great, but in reality, when it does give sources, even when you ask for it, links and citations are often poor quality such as a reddit post, not fully related to the prompt, or even flat out broken and lead nowhere.

While I know this will improve over time, it leaves me with more questions than answers:

  • Who ultimately decides what "sources" to use and train models on?

  • Similar to a search engine results, how much can the LLM be biased in favor of a specific narrative the model creators want?

  • Can you patent what comes out of an LLM without using a source?

  • How would you even know it's lying? This assumes you know more than or have easy access to alternative information which is validated. In that case, why would you ask the bot?

Illusion of Power

Imagine using an LLM to make a new product or piece of content, do you really want to see a list of all the sources and all related patents for every prompt?

The power to generate something fast comes with responsibility. Seeing the sources would mean your history of interacting with the LLM could hold you accountable for plagiarism or patent infringement, even if unintentional.

And yes, everything you prompt an LLM with is potentially saved long-term. It is not held to the same security standard as other systems are, like a bank or medical data for example, at least not yet. So if you freely give them your medical info, PII, or evidence for a crime, that's on you.

If you do something illegal, or someone just claims you did something illegal and you go to court, they will try to query for it and it will become public information if ruled. This has already happened, and I doubt it will be the last time.

It's too much like giving a chainsaw to a child for a government to just ignore. People just willingly enter their deepest and darkest thoughts they would never share otherwise.

The idea that you can ask whatever you want from a LLM secretly and you can do whatever you want will always be an illusion. The same goes for anything on the freaking internet. You do not own the AI or the data center, a corporation does. If you really want this "power" for yourself, you have to go and build the infrastructure yourself and run it locally.

Those selling you a subscription to use their pet robot for a monthly fee don't want the credit to go anywhere else. Just like any business, investors don't want heavy competition or for their product to be subject to complexity, they want a super deep moat.

Alpaca Giraffe Iguana

I'm not against AI. See the image for this post above, I generated a "funny" image, so my stonks number will go up. Am I cool now? /s

It's not just image generators and LLMs. There are so many other applications for AI that are not often talked about in the news, and nobody really knows what the real limitations and consequences will be.

I doubt super intelligence is on the radar anytime soon, if ever, but if I am wrong and some god-tier AI tool or worse emerged, do you really think the model itself, the company who created it, or a Government welding it will care about you, your prompts, or your SaaS project?
