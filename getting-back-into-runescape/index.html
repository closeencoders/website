Old Dog, New Tricks: How RuneScape Stays Relevant in a Modern Era

Whether RuneScape is truly charming or just nostalgia is hard to say, but its recent updates, RuneLite plugins, and popularity speak for themselves.

By Josh - Apr 24, 2026 | gaming

RuneScape. An ancient MMO and a massive part of my childhood, right up there with Halo 3 and Pokémon. I’m not alone in this feeling either, I have found this to be common among nerdy people roughly my age. But why would anyone be playing this game in 2026?

On the surface RuneScape looks and feels basic, and it’s difficult to pin down exactly what gives it charm beyond nostalgia through rose-colored glasses. Yet, the game still attracts new players and maintains an active community. It even appears to be growing, and there are some possible reasons why it has made a comeback.

For one, several popular streamers gave RuneScape a try recently. Because of this, a broader audience has become aware of the game with the desire to try it. While the streams have died down on Twitch, RuneScape’s popularity for content creation on YouTube is still there.

The sudden interest may have given the game the second wind that it needed. Many players who had been there since the mid-2000s didn’t enjoy what RuneScape had become. Upon the re-release of the 2007 version of RuneScape, or Old School RuneScape (OSRS), players were funneled back in time and had to start over from level 3. Many people quit at that point, but others have returned recently due to nostalgia and the recent surge in popularity.

Another point, the Jagex development team has been on a winning streak for the past few years with new content and expanding the game to mobile and Steam. Players feel they have a say in the game’s direction through polls, barring a few exceptions. Jagex is even releasing a new Sailing skill, which I am eager to try. Along with solid development, alternative clients and launchers like RuneLite and Bolt have become popular and well-supported, resulting in the game being more accessible to a larger audience thanks to helpful plugins and quality-of-life improvements.

Lastly, RuneScape has a reward system and game loop that tilts toward delayed gratification and horizontal progression. At least within most aspects of OSRS, when you achieve something, you can come back later (often years), and it still matters. This is a stark contrast to many modern games, or even just day-to-day activities in the modern world, sadly. Reaching a max level, let alone a maxed account, can take an extremely long time, but the game never forces you to max out just to have a good time, either.

A Second Wind

Knowing a bit of history helps with understanding the split between RS3 and OSRS and which one a player might want to try. It also explains why OSRS was in the right place at the right time. In roughly 1999, Andrew Gower created a prototype game called DeviousMUD. This eventually evolved into the browser-based Java version of the original RuneScape in January 2001.

Yes, you read that right. RuneScape’s origin story is from a previous century and has exceeded just about all expectations for the lifespan of an MMO of its kind.

It just refuses to die.

Over a decade after the game's inception, in November 2012, Jagex released the Evolution of Combat (EoC), which nearly killed the game, a demise most games meet after enough time passes and the executives behind the game lose touch with reality. The update replaced traditional mechanics with an ability-bar system that completely changed how the game dynamics worked.

It’s not that the update was exceedingly bad or anything, but it went against what players had come to expect of RuneScape. It was off-brand to say the least.

The community backlash was severe enough that Jagex restored a 2007 version of the game, creating the split between RS3 and OSRS that exists today. This followed an earlier era of unrest in late 2007, when the removal of Free Trade and the Wilderness PvP zone led to massive player protests until their eventual return.

The game lived on, but had taken some damage.

These two versions of the game that offer vastly different experiences. RS3 is often criticized for its “gambling” feel due to microtransactions like Treasure Hunter (much of which was removed thankfully), whereas OSRS remains more focused on the original grind.

Fast-forward to the present, and OSRS has been hit with a wave of streamers and nostalgia-fueled returning players just as it was gaining traction from solid updates and development. This was partly due to World of Warcraft having frustrated a portion of its own player base, many stating they were fed up with the treadmill and the feeling that their previous achievements had no meaning after enough time passed.

Still Developed and Supported With Customization

You might think with the age of the game that support and updates would be limited, but to my own surprise, the Jagex dev team still pushes relevant updates and asks the community what they want through polls for new features and tweaks.

The most recent large scale change is the Sailing skill. Another is the addition to the Fletching skill, a minigame called Vale Totems, which included some training methods involving spirit animals. It’s ‘trippy’ and provides a nice change-up from other various bank-standing ways to train fetching.

I addition, if you have been gone a very long time, you can also play the game through Steam or on mobile for both OSRS and RS3. It’s not perfect, but they did a pretty good job, and it’s very playable.

Possible Enhancements Based on Preference

There is no ‘best’ or ‘right’ way to play any game, especially RuneScape. Everyone plays the game differently, and that’s 100% okay.

However, for those who don’t know anything about the game, the average person may get a bad taste in their mouth in the early stages because some aspects of the game are not obvious. This is magnified for younger players, the older-looking UI and not knowing you can change or customize it pushes people away fast.

Even if they manage to look past the UI, there is often confusion about what to do after leaving the tutorial island. The intro does a decent job of showing you some mechanics, but it does not give you a major set of goals or direction. While this is one of the biggest pros of the game (aka freedom) it may not feel like a good thing to someone coming from other games or just a random person trying it for the first time.

If the player is still logged in after that, and forges their own path, if they pick a ‘cringy’ starting quest or try to train a difficult skill like Slayer without using something like the wiki, runehq, or at least some videos to show them where to go, it’s easy to assume the entire game will be this annoying and difficult.

As a veteran, this is a cakewalk, but without help or guidance from somebody who has played before to aid you, you may have to navigate several days or so to see if it’s actually fun. If you have patience and have played other MMOs, you may be more willing to push through, knowing it will get better. I would argue that many don’t though. Especially if they subject themselves to free-to-play worlds killing tons of chickens or cows in a horde of bots for longer than necessary, only to be slapped in the face with anti-bot Grand Exchange (GE) restrictions or a weird quest requirement.

The initial no-reward game loop can feel awful.

So if you are impatient, then I am only proposing ways to improve the experience that you could choose from if they sound good to you.

1. Modern UI and A Different Client Like RuneLite

One of, if not the most important thing to know is that playing OSRS with the default client instead of something like RuneLite can be a major handicap. Unless you specifically want a “raw” old-school vibe, it won’t be as enjoyable for most people.

Once installed, use the drop-down menu to select the RuneLite client in your launcher. I recommend watching a video or two to see how to customize it to your liking and make the experience less annoying.

The main benefits are the plugins, everything from graphics improvements and item highlighting to quality-of-life perks. The list of improvements feels endless and can make the game feel like it belongs in the current decade.

At a minimum, I recommend doing the following:

  • If you don’t like the default UI, then Go to settings and change the UI to the modern layout and enable scaling/resizing. This pairs well with RuneLite and other visual plugins.
  • If you hate the default graphics, then Install 117 HD Plugin.
  • If you are extra impatient and hate wikis, then install Quest Helper Plugin.
  • RuneLite might have automatic screenshots for achievements/levels on by default; if you hate that, you can disable it.

Here is a side-by-side example. While it might not seem like much to those used to modern GPUs, after several hours of play, it makes a huge difference for your eyes.

2. Don't Play as an Ironman As Your First Character

Playing in Ironman mode is fun, but it limits player-to-player interaction. If you don't want to play solo, meaning you literally cannot trade at all, it can be a terrible idea for a first character.

Once you understand the game better, it becomes a fun challenge and a flex, but as a newbie, it can feel isolating. It makes many of the skills go from hundreds of hours to several thousand to achieve 99s for a normal person with a life.

If you absolutely feel the need, at the very least, do group Ironman with friends. Most will burn out or regret the decision when you hit a quest requirement that needs an Item you cant buy and is only attainable from some crazy niche grind.

3. Shift-click Drop Items and Keybinds

There is a setting that lets you drop items quickly. It’s not dependent on RuneLite, it’s built into the game. In the settings, just check “Shift click drop items”. Now you can quickly empty your inventory if you need to.

I cannot tell you how upset I was when I found out this was a thing. I was right-clicking for days and wondering how people were dropping their entire inventory in a few seconds or less.

Just… Uhhhh!

4. Do Easy Quests With Experience Rewards Sooner Rather Than Later

If you plan to do quests, consider starting them at soon as possible. Especially if they reward XP in skills you don’t like, but need to raise your level.

A great example would be the Museum quiz in Varrock. It’s kind of boring, but it’s short and gives good XP (if you are a lower level) for Hunter and Slayer skills. You can then dodge the early-level grind for those skills, as they become much, MUCH better after level 10 to 20+.

If you wait too long and power level your skills, the amount of experience you get from the quests at a higher level does not feel worth the time.

A lot of the starting quests and miniquests, including the free-to-play quests, will boost your stats without you having to do the early grind from level one in many different skills. Going right into Hunter, Agility, or Runecrafting without some quests can feel like getting punched in the face.

The entire game doesn’t have to feel that way, and doing a few quests alleviates the early grind BS if you want it to. Maybe you will love starting Hunter from level one, and it will be your favorite skill.

5. You Can Organize Your Bank

New players often don't notice you can organize your bank, down to an excruciating level of detail if desired. Making new tabs and dragging and dropping items in the manner you want in your bank makes the experience of collecting items for quests and tasks less frustrating.

There are also plugins that will help automate or improve your bank as well if using RuneLite. My bank is not super organized at the moment, but even a little bit helps in the long run.

6. (Skippable Bonus) - If You Play on Linux

The only reasons I logout of Debian and launch Microsoft Windows on my dual-booted machine is for gaming and work. But you can now play OSRS on Linux with ease. Granted, you may need to use a third party launcher if you are not inclined to tinker.

I am currently using Bolt and RuneLite through Flatpak. I know that some hate Flatpak, but that is the easiest way to get the game up and going, otherwise play around with Wine and graphics settings.

Wine has become much better over the years, but I don’t prefer Wine for RuneScape.

Game Loop

As far as MMOs go, OSRS is a master class at horizontal progression and respecting the ‘grind’.

Because of this, when you earn something in OSRS, it often carries real meaning and weight, and it will stay that way years into the future. When you complete that quest, unlock a new area or a nifty piece of armor, you feel it. Then, when you login six months later, it's still there and most likely just as useful as the day you unlocked it.

There isn’t a class system either, and you can do just about anything you want whenever you want, barring level and quest requirements for obvious things.

Want to make an account, then grind only cooking to 99 for no reason, and just walk around making jokes? You can.

Want to be a strength pure, never do more than a quest or two, and go around trying to kill people and bots in the wilderness just to s–t post on YouTube for fun? Go for it! It can be quite profitable too.

There is no main quest line that is required to play the game. For example, many players make accounts called “skillers” with only the intention of leveling up non-combat skills because they find it therapeutic.

You can play however you want.

Final Thoughts

RuneScape is not a perfect game, and it's not for everyone. This can be said for any game, but I think it does have a weird charm to it, and is worth a try if you enjoy sandbox-style MMOs. Everything from its art style to audio is iconic, and its progression system sets it apart from most games in the genre. If the game feels too basic, just remember to give RuneLite a try, there may be a plugin that fixes an issue you can't stand about the game.
